Singer's Tomb Unearthed in Egypt

A fascinating discovery in Egypt's magnificent Valley of the Kings! Archaeologists have unearthed a 3000-year-old tomb of a female singer, it is the only tomb of a woman not related to the ancient Egyptian royal families that has ever been found there, said Mansour Boraik, the top government official for the Antiquities Ministry in the city of Luxor. The coffin of the female singer, remarkably intact, is said to be Nehmes Bastet, and believed to be protected by the feline deity Bastet - seen as the defender of the pharaoh. The tomb was found by accident, according to Elina Paulin-Grothe, Field Director of the excavation at the Valley of the Kings with Switzerland's University of Basel. Archaeologists have concluded from artifacts that she sang in the glorious Karnak Temple, one of the most famous and largest open-air sites from the Pharaonic era.

Jerash Festival Is Back!

The Jerash Festival for Culture and Arts is back again for 2012. The festival will run from July 4th until July 29th and will feature music, poetry, theatre and dance performances by prominent artists from 30 different countries, including Jordan and the Arab regions.

Since 1981, the old city of Jerash has been hosting this major international festival, a three week long summer program of dance, music, and theatrical performances. The festival is frequently attended by members of the royal family of Jordan and is hailed as one of the largest cultural activities in the region.

This year, Jerash Festival will take place in two different places; From July the 4th till July the 14th it will take place in Jerash, and from July the 23rd till July the 29th it will take place in Royal Cultural Center.

Inaugurated by Queen Noor Al-Hussein, the festival has become the premiere showcase for Jordanian performing artists. The ancient Greco-Roman City provides an unforgettable backdrop to this cultural carnival, and this year the venues include the North and South Theatres, the colonnaded streets, Byzantine churches and the steps of the Artemis Temple, all within walking distance of modern Jerash.

The Jerash Festival takes place during a traditionally quiet season for tourism in Jordan, (July - August) making it easy to book hotel rooms and air travel at attractive prices. So, don't miss the chance to enjoy this unforgettable experience!

Archaeologist's friends remember Father Piccirillo

Dignitaries, researchers and friends from across the region gathered this month to pay tribute to the person many regard to be the "godfather" of Jordanian mosaics. A Franciscan father, professor, archaeologist and peace-builder, Abuna (Father) Piccirillo was a man known to wear many hats.

Michele Piccirillo left behind a body of work that forced scholars and clerics alike to rethink historical coexistence between Christians and Muslims in the holy lands, proving that Christian communities flourished during the zenith of the Umayyad era.

Kunafeh is now officially part of the 'Jordan experience'

Confectioners report that kunafeh has become a must-buy treat for tourists when they visit Amman on account of its popularity in the Kingdom. Kunafeh is an Arabic sweet, made of very fine shreds of pastry that is layered with soft cheese and topped with sugary syrup.

Since the early days of modern Amman, Jordanians have become very attached to kunafeh, serving it during national celebrations and at all kinds of social events. During the 1930s, Jordanians used to prepare this dish at home, however, with the Palestinian influx after the 1948 Arab-Israeli several families arrived specialising in making kunafeh. Throughout the 40s different sweet stores opened up such as Abu Seir, Shahrazad and Habibah. Since then, these shops have attracted Jordanians and visitors alike, with various sweet offerings. And people keep coming back for more!
