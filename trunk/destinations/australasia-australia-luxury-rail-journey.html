Luxury Rail Journey

The Ghan
(Adelaide - Alice Springs - Darwin)

The Ghan. It's an odd name for a train, but in Australian history it is a living legend, for it is the ultimate journey through the heart of the continent. After countless attempts by man and beast to break through the waterless desert into the red centre, a bright South Australian farmer came up with an excellent idea. A hundred and fifty years ago the first camels were imported along with their handlers from Afghanistan and Aussies soon shortened their name to "Ghan." Early explorers and later the telegraph linesman used camels, perfectly adapted for travelling long distances through the hot, inhospitable interior, to blaze a permanent trail into Central Australia. When the first Ghan train departed Adelaide on 4 August 1929, it was always intended that it would one day travel through to Darwin. More than 70 years on, that dream has become reality. Boarding The Ghan in Adelaide for Darwin, with overnight connections on the Indian Pacific and The Overland for guests travelling to or from Sydney and Melbourne, guests will experience one of the most fascinating great train journeys of the world, with 2 nights aboard this great train.

Marvel at the spectacular Australian landscapes; from the rusty reds of the MacDonnell Ranges surrounding a town called Alice, gateway to Ayers Rock and the Red centre, then north to Tennant Creek, Katherine and the tropical splendour of Darwin. Off-train touring opportunities along the way provide the chance to explore the landscapes and encounter some of the unique desert flora and fauna.

Indian Pacific
(Sydney - Adelaide - Perth)

As the name implies, the Indian Pacific cruises between two oceans on one of the world's longest and greatest train journeys. However, most of the cruising is across the vast continent of Australia. From the spectacular Blue Mountains to the treeless plains of The Nullarbor, guests see unique landscapes unfold and spot a fascinating array of wildlife from the comfort of the lounge or sleeper cabin.

The journey from Sydney to Perth covers 4352 km in either direction and includes 3 nights aboard the Indian Pacific. There are many things to see along the way as the Indian Pacific crosses this vast continent. The Indian Pacific offers optional tours during scheduled stops at Broken Hill, Adelaide and gold rich Kalgoorlie. A remote outpost on the Nullarbor Plain called Cook (population 4) presents a further opportunity for guests to stretch their legs. All along the Indian Pacific route, or "IP" as it is affectionately known to rail enthusiasts, there are plenty of chances for a sighting of the magnificent Australian wedge tailed eagle, the symbol of this great train.

The Southern Spirit
(Adelaide/Melbourne - Brisbane)

The latest in Australia's Great Train Journeys is departing for its second season in 2011. Travelling between Adelaide/Melbourne and Brisbane. The most recent addition to our fleet was named in honour of the spirit of rail adventure, held in the hearts of those men and women responsible for opening up the far reaches of this great continent. Follow the pass less travelled in Platinum or Gold Service as The Southern Spirit explores the sights and delights of Australia's southeast.

The Southern Spirit will take guests from Adelaide - the gateway to the Australian outback - on a journey of discovery through four states, as it winds its way through hills and ranges, then skirts the coast destined for the sub tropical city of Brisbane. Over six unforgettable days, unwind and share in a journey filled with the essence of Australia's south east. Including Whistle Stop Tours at selected locations en route, The Southern Spirit highlights the essence of Australia's southeast including The Grampians National Park, Ned Kelly Country, and the majestic Murray River. Visit the Western Plains then cross the awe inspiring Blue Mountains and descend to the Hunter Valley for an extended tour. Enjoy a taste of the pristine mid-north coast of New South Wales, with visits to Port Macquarie and Byron Bay.
