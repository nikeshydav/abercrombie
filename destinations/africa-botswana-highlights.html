Botswana Highlights & Sightseeing

  • Okavango Delta

    Okavango Delta is the largest inland delta in the world situated right in the heart of Botswana. Its waters don't flow into the sea but bring about an annual flood of fresh waters in the inland, over some 15000 km of the Kalahari Desert sand.

    Starting at Angola's western highlands, it flows in the form of lagoons and channels and makes numerous tributaries that join to form the Cubango River. This river then flows via Namibia to Botswana and is called the Okavango here.

    Moremi Game Reserve

    Moremi Game Reserve is a 3000km² paradise situated in the centre of the Okavango Delta. It was found by the Batawana tribe and was ascribed to be a quintessentially pure wildlife reserve by the Tswana people in 1963. The reserve covers a grand 4871km²area with the richness of its wilderness well-maintained where you can even manage to get a peek into the divine Garden of Eden. With nature's bounty in surplus, this game reserve has abundance of lily-covered wetlands, grass plains and forest-covers which bring to light some of the most amazing wildlife moments.

    Moremi has interplay of a network of waterways which surround two large islands, namely the Chief's island in the West and Mopane Tongue in the East. You can witness some of the most spectacular wildlife views combined with a journey into the wetlands, reed beds, mopane forests and dry savannahs while experiencing some of the rarest wildlife sightings

    The Chobe River

    Running along the northern border of the Chobe National Park, the Chobe River is a rare sight. Rising in the northern Angolan highlands where it's referred to as the 'Kwando' which is a Hambukushu name, it travels to great lengths to reach Botswana via Kalahari Desert.

    Until it reaches Ngoma, its called Linyati (a Subiya name) after which it's finally known as the Chobe. Flowing in a similar pattern as the Okavango and Zambezi, it's afflicted with a lot of rough courses in its path making the extensions of East Africa's Great Rift Valley. These three rivers combined carry more water than any other source in Southern Africa.

    Chobe National Park

    The Chobe National Park is the second largest National Park in Botswana covering a massive land area of 10566km². Strategic location allows for diverse wildlife to co-exist making it one of the richest reserves in Africa. Divided into four distinct eco-systems, this park offers a great safari experience for wildlife lovers.

    When on one hand you have Serondela with thick forest cover and lush green plains in the Chobe River area in the extreme north-east, you have Savuti Marsh to complement it for around fifty kilometres in the west on the north of Mababe gate. There is also the Linyati Swamps in the north-eastern region which has a filling of hot and dry woodland in between.

    The Kalahari Desert

    The famed Kalahari Desert is very much present here giving a tangy twist to Botswana. It's not a real-desert but a semi-arid zone which extends from the Orange River in South Africa to the Southern edge of Congo. Kalahari covers a massive 70% land area in Botswana and is home to the famous tribe of San (Bushmen) who are known for their rock paintings.

    The Desert is home to hyenas, lions, meerkats, antelope of various kinds and several types of birds and reptiles. Mostly covered with dry grassland and scrubby acacias, Kalahari sees itself covered in grass only during the rainy season in summers. Threatening the existing civilisation in Kalahari, modern ways of living are penetrating into the desert land and today only a few San follow their traditional way of life.
